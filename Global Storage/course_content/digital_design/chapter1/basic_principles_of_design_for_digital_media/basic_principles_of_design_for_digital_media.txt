Understanding and applying these principles is crucial for any designer working in digital media, regardless of the specific medium.</p>

    <h1 id="hierarchy" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">1. Hierarchy</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Hierarchy guides the viewer's eye through the design, prioritizing information by using visual cues like size, color, and contrast.  It establishes a clear order of importance, making it easy for users to understand the content and navigate the interface.</p>

    <h1 id="balance" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">2. Balance</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Balance refers to the distribution of visual weight within a design.  It can be symmetrical (elements mirrored across a central axis) or asymmetrical (elements balanced through visual weight and placement).  Balance creates a sense of stability and harmony.</p>


    <h1 id="alignment" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">3. Alignment</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Alignment creates a sense of order and connection between elements.  By aligning elements to a common axis, you create a clean and organized look.  Alignment can be left, right, center, or justified.</p>


    <h1 id="emphasis" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">4. Emphasis</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Emphasis highlights key elements within a design, drawing the viewer's attention to the most important information.  Techniques for creating emphasis include using contrasting colors, larger font sizes, or isolating elements with whitespace.</p>

    <h1 id="proportion" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">5. Proportion</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Proportion refers to the relative size and scale of elements within a design.  It creates visual harmony and balance by establishing relationships between different elements. The Golden Ratio is a classic example of proportion in design.</p>

    <h1 id="movement" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">6. Movement</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Movement guides the viewer's eye through the design, creating a sense of flow and direction.  Techniques for creating movement include using diagonal lines, curves, and directional cues.</p>

    <h1 id="negative-space" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">7. Negative Space (Whitespace)</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Negative space, or whitespace, is the empty space around and between elements in a design. It's crucial for readability and visual clarity.  It gives elements room to breathe and prevents the design from feeling cluttered.</p>

    <h1 id="contrast" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">8. Contrast</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Contrast creates visual interest and helps to differentiate elements within a design.  It can be achieved through differences in color, size, shape, texture, or typography.  High contrast makes elements stand out, while low contrast creates a more subtle effect.</p>


    <h1 id="repetition" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">9. Repetition</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Repetition creates a sense of unity and consistency.  By repeating elements like colors, fonts, or shapes, you establish a visual rhythm and reinforce the overall design theme.</p>


    <h1 id="variety" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">10. Variety</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Variety adds visual interest and prevents a design from becoming monotonous.  It can be achieved by using different colors, fonts, shapes, and sizes.  Variety should be used in moderation to avoid creating a chaotic or disjointed design.</p>

    <h1 id="unity" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">11. Unity</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Unity is the sense of harmony and coherence between all elements in a design.  It's achieved by using the other principles of design effectively to create a cohesive and visually pleasing whole.  A unified design feels complete and well-integrated.</p>


    <h1 id="proximity" class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2 mt-8">12. Proximity</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Proximity groups related elements together, creating a sense of organization and visual connection.  Elements placed close together are perceived as belonging to the same group, while elements placed further apart are seen as separate entities.</p>
