    <h1 class="text-3xl font-bold mb-4">Mitigation Strategies: Forks and Airdrops</h1>

    <h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2">Hard Forks</h2>
    <p class="mb-4">A hard fork is a radical change to a blockchain protocol that makes previously invalid blocks/transactions valid (or vice-versa). This creates a permanent divergence from the previous version of the blockchain, effectively creating a new cryptocurrency.  If you hold the original coin, you typically receive an equal amount of the new coin. The tax implications of a hard fork can be complex and depend on how it's treated by tax authorities.</p>


    <h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2">Soft Forks</h2>
    <p class="mb-4">A soft fork is a change to the blockchain protocol that's backward-compatible.  Older nodes on the network will continue to function, but they may not enforce the new rules. Soft forks generally do not result in the creation of a new cryptocurrency. Their tax implications are usually simpler than those of hard forks.</p>

    <h2 class="text-2xl font-bold mb-2">Airdrops</h2>
    <p class="mb-4">An airdrop is a distribution of a cryptocurrency, typically for free, to numerous wallet addresses. Airdrops are often used as a marketing strategy to promote new projects or reward existing holders of a particular cryptocurrency. Airdrops are generally considered taxable income at the time of receipt, based on the fair market value of the received tokens.</p>



    <h1 class="text-3xl font-bold mb-4">Crypto Derivatives</h1>
    <p class="mb-4">Crypto derivatives are financial instruments whose value is derived from the price of an underlying cryptocurrency.  Examples include futures contracts and options. These can be complex instruments with significant leverage, posing both opportunities and risks for traders.</p>
